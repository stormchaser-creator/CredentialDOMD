## 1. DEA Registration Lapse (Urgency: Critical)

**The problem:** DEA registration renews every 3 years -- long enough that physicians forget, short enough that it comes around fast. A lapsed DEA means you *cannot prescribe controlled substances*, which for most specialties means you cannot practice. There's no grace period. Re-registration after lapse triggers additional scrutiny and delays. Many physicians only discover the lapse when a pharmacist rejects a prescription -- in front of a patient.

**Hook:** *"If your DEA lapses on a Friday, you can't write a single controlled substance script on Monday -- and most physicians don't know theirs expires until a pharmacist calls."*

---

## 2. Multi-State License Renewal Chaos (Urgency: High)

**The problem:** A locum physician holding 3-5 state licenses faces 3-5 different renewal dates, 3-5 different CME requirement sets (some require state-specific topics like opioid education or pain management hours), and 3-5 different portals with different login credentials. Miss one renewal and you lose the ability to practice in that state -- which means cancelled locum assignments and lost income, often $5K-$15K per week. The Interstate Medical Licensure Compact helps initial licensure but does *not* synchronize renewals.

**Hook:** *"You're juggling five state licenses with five different renewal dates and five different CME rules -- and one missed deadline costs you a $10K locum week."*

## 3. Board Certification MOC/OCC Cycles (Urgency: High)

**The problem:** ABMS board certification now requires continuous participation in Maintenance of Certification (MOC) or Ongoing Certification and Competency (OCC) programs -- but the requirements differ by specialty board, the cycle lengths vary (5-10 years with interim checkpoints), and the consequences of falling behind are severe. Many hospitals and insurers now require *active* board certification for credentialing and network participation. Physicians often don't realize they've fallen out of compliance until a hospital flags it during re-credentialing -- which can mean suspension of privileges.

**Hook:** *"Your hospital won't tell you your MOC lapsed -- they'll tell you your privileges are suspended during your next re-credentialing cycle."*

## 4. CAQH ProView: The Unfunded Mandate (Urgency: Moderate-High)

**The problem:** CAQH ProView is required by virtually every payer for credentialing, but it's designed for *health plans and employers*, not physicians. The interface is hostile -- dozens of screens, redundant data entry, attestation every 120 days or your profile goes inactive. An inactive CAQH profile means payers can't credential you, which means claims get denied. Physicians spend 8-20 hours per year maintaining CAQH, and most don't realize their profile has gone inactive until revenue stops flowing. The 120-day re-attestation window is the silent killer.

**Hook:** *"CAQH gives you 120 days to re-attest or your profile goes dark -- and you won't know until insurance claims start bouncing."*

---

## 5. MATE Act Compliance Gap (Urgency: Moderate, Rising)

**The problem:** The Consolidation Appropriations Act of 2023 (MATE Act) eliminated the X-waiver but *added* a requirement: all DEA-registered prescribers must complete 8 hours of opioid-related education as a one-time condition of DEA registration/renewal. Many physicians assume that since the X-waiver is gone, there are no additional requirements. They're wrong. Non-compliance puts your DEA registration at risk -- which circles back to Pain Point #1. The requirement is relatively new, enforcement is tightening, and most physicians haven't confirmed whether their existing CME already satisfies it.

**Hook:** *"The X-waiver is gone, but the MATE Act quietly added 8 hours of opioid education to your DEA renewal -- and most physicians don't know they owe it."*
