Here's the full competitive landscape analysis for physician credential management — written from a CredentialDOMD positioning lens.

## The Fundamental Market Divide

Every existing tool was built for **institutions** — hospitals, health plans, credentialing verification organizations. They manage *pools* of physicians as assets to be verified. Not one of them was built for the physician as the primary user and owner of their own credential data. That gap is where CredentialDOMD lives.

---

## Competitor-by-Competitor Breakdown

### 1. CAQH ProView
**Who it serves:** Health plans and payers. CAQH is a non-profit consortium *owned by the insurance industry* — United, Aetna, BCBS et al. The physician doesn't choose CAQH; they're mandated into it to get paid.

**Pricing:** Free to physicians to *enter* data. Real cost is 4–12 hours of the physician's time per setup, plus $80–$240/yr in administrative labor for ongoing re-attestation every 120 days. Third-party setup services charge $50–$250/provider. Total lifecycle cost: **$3,000–$7,000/year** when all admin overhead is counted.

**Key limitation from a physician's perspective:**
- The physician is the *data entry clerk*, not the customer. CAQH sells that data to payers.
- Data is siloed inside CAQH's network — it doesn't help you credential at a new hospital, apply to a locum tenens agency, or update your CV.
- Mandatory re-attestation every 120 days, even if nothing changed.
- Zero physician-facing intelligence — no expiration alerts, no gap analysis, no "what do I need to apply to X."
- You don't own your profile; CAQH does.

---

### 2. Modio Health (OneView)
**Who it serves:** Healthcare organizations — group practices, health systems, staffing companies managing pools of clinicians. The *buyer* is a credentialing director or HR department, not the physician.

**Pricing:** Custom enterprise pricing, typically **$150+/provider/month** — billed to the organization. Individual physicians cannot purchase it.

**Key limitation from a physician's perspective:**
- Individual physicians cannot buy or access it directly.
- A physician's OneView profile belongs to the organization that paid for the license. When they leave, they lose access to *their own data*.
- Built for credentialing department workflows (task tracking, verification queues, compliance reporting) — not for physician self-management.
- No portability: your data doesn't follow you across employers.

---

### 3. Symplr (formerly Cactus Software)
**Who it serves:** Large hospital systems and health plans. Trusted by **9 out of 10 U.S. hospitals** and 400+ health plans — the most dominant enterprise player in the space.

**Pricing:** Custom enterprise contracts. Estimate: **$50,000–$500,000+/year** for health system deployments. Not available to individuals at any price.

**Key limitation from a physician's perspective:**
- Explicitly an enterprise product — hospitals *use* it to manage physicians as workforce assets.
- The physician is the *subject* of the software, not the user. They may fill out an application in a Symplr portal, but they have no ongoing account, no access to their data, and no control over it.
- 9,600+ privilege templates, OPPE performance tracking, HR integration — all features for hospital administration, zero features for the physician's own career management.
- If a physician works at 3 hospitals, they exist as 3 separate records in 3 separate Symplr installations, none of which talk to each other.

---

### 4. MD-Staff
**Who it serves:** Medical staff offices — the department inside a hospital that manages provider credentialing, privileging, and enrollment. Used by 2,000+ facilities worldwide.

**Pricing:** Quote-based, subscription model. Estimated **$15,000–$100,000+/year** depending on facility size. Not available to individuals.

**Key limitation from a physician's perspective:**
- Same fundamental issue: MD-Staff is *for* the medical staff office, not the physician. Physicians interact with it only as applicants filling out forms.
- AI automation (their "Aiva" engine) is oriented toward reducing the hospital's administrative burden, not the physician's.
- No cross-facility portability. A physician applying to a new hospital starts from zero.
- No alerts, career intelligence, or gap analysis for the physician themselves.

---

### 5. Spreadsheets / Manual Tracking (The Actual Incumbent)
**Who it serves:** Individual physicians managing their own credentials — the *real* dominant tool for physician self-management today.

**Pricing:** "Free" (paid in time and anxiety). Reality: an average credentialing application requires **~30 documents per provider**, with a dozen or more having expiration dates. A physician managing 2–3 active hospital privileges, DEA registration, state licenses in multiple states, board certifications, and malpractice history can easily spend **8–15 hours per year** just maintaining the tracking overhead — at physician opportunity cost rates.

**Key limitation from a physician's perspective:**
- No expiration alerts — licenses lapse because no one was watching.
- Re-entering the same data (NPI, DEA, residency dates, malpractice carrier) for every new application from scratch.
- No document storage that follows you across jobs.
- Zero intelligence: a spreadsheet can't tell you "your Pennsylvania license expires in 47 days" or "you're missing a document to apply to this hospital network."
- Catastrophic when physicians transition between employers, add a locum position, or face a surprise re-credentialing audit.
- Data lives on a laptop, not anywhere portable or secure.

## Why CredentialDOMD Is Categorically Different

| Dimension | All Existing Tools | CredentialDOMD |
|---|---|---|
| **Primary customer** | Hospital / payer / organization | The physician |
| **Data ownership** | Institution owns the profile | Physician owns the profile |
| **Portability** | Locked to one employer/network | Data follows the physician everywhere |
| **Intelligence** | None for the physician | Expiration tracking, gap analysis, application readiness |
| **Individual access** | Not available or not the point | Built specifically for individual physicians |
| **Business model** | Sell physician data to payers (CAQH) or sell workflows to HR departments | Subscription to the physician |
| **Value created for physician** | Compliance burden (data entry) | Active career asset management |

**The core insight:** Every competitor treats physician credentials as a *risk management problem for institutions*. CredentialDOMD treats physician credentials as a *career asset for the physician*.

When a physician uses CredentialDOMD:
- They enter their data once, they own it, and it persists across every job they ever hold.
- They get proactive alerts before anything expires — no more scrambling.
- They can generate a complete, current credential packet for any new credentialing application in minutes rather than days.
- They can see gaps before a hospital's credentialing office sees them.
- Their data is never sold to a payer or locked inside an employer's HR system.

**The incumbent to beat isn't Symplr or CAQH — it's the Google Sheet on the physician's desktop.** The sales motion isn't "we're better than Symplr"; it's "you shouldn't be managing your medical license with a spreadsheet and hoping nothing expires while you're seeing patients."
