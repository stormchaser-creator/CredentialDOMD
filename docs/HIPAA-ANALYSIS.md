## Threshold Question: What Kind of Entity Is CredentialDOMD?

HIPAA governs two categories of regulated parties under **45 CFR § 160.103**:

**Covered Entities (CE)** — health plans, health care clearinghouses, and health care providers who electronically transmit health information in HIPAA standard transactions (claims, eligibility, remittance).

**Business Associates (BA)** — persons or entities that create, receive, maintain, or transmit *protected health information* (PHI) on behalf of a covered entity.

CredentialDOMD is a SaaS platform. It is not a health plan, not a clearinghouse, and not itself a health care provider. **It is not a covered entity.**

Whether it is a *business associate* turns entirely on a prior question: **Is physician credential data PHI?**

---

### 1. Is Physician Credential Data PHI?

**Determination: No. Physician credential data is not PHI under 45 CFR § 160.103.**

PHI is defined as **individually identifiable health information** that meets *all three* of the following prongs:

| Prong | Requirement | Analysis |
|---|---|---|
| **Subject matter** | Must relate to: (a) a physical or mental health *condition*; (b) *provision of health care*; or (c) *payment* for health care | ❌ Fails |
| **Origin/custody** | Created or received by a CE or BA | N/A (fails on prong 1) |
| **Identifiability** | Identifies or could reasonably identify an individual | N/A |

The data CredentialDOMD stores — medical licenses, DEA registrations, board certifications, hospital privileges, CME records, and malpractice history — describes a physician's **professional qualifications and regulatory standing**, not any patient's health condition, health care received, or payment for care. The data *subjects* are the physicians themselves, acting in their professional capacity.

**Key distinctions under 45 CFR § 160.103:**

- The "individual" HIPAA protects is implicitly the **patient** (the person receiving health care). The entire regulatory scheme — Notice of Privacy Practices, minimum necessary, access rights — is built around this premise. Credential data never touches this category.
- "Provision of health care" in this definition means care *delivered to a patient*, not the professional activities *enabling* a physician to practice.
- Malpractice history in standard credentialing is presented as aggregate/administrative data (claim counts, settlement ranges, final dispositions) — not as patient-identified case records. If a physician were to upload actual patient records as supporting documentation, *that subset* would be PHI. The metadata itself is not.

**Confirming authority:** HHS Office for Civil Rights guidance consistently frames PHI as information about individuals *as patients*, not as licensed professionals. Credentialing information is explicitly treated as administrative/professional data in the NPDB (National Practitioner Data Bank) framework under **42 CFR Part 60**, which operates independently of HIPAA.

**Therefore: CredentialDOMD is neither a covered entity nor a business associate under HIPAA.** The BA definition under **45 CFR § 160.103** requires that the entity handle PHI on behalf of a CE. No PHI, no BA status.

### 2. Does CredentialDOMD Need a BAA with Supabase?

**Determination: Not required. Strongly recommended as a strategic posture.**

A BAA is only mandated under **45 CFR § 164.504(e)** when a covered entity or business associate discloses PHI to a downstream entity. Since CredentialDOMD's core data isn't PHI, the BAA obligation doesn't trigger.

**However**, three practical considerations push toward executing a BAA anyway:

1. **Scope creep risk.** If CredentialDOMD later adds features — peer references, clinical outcomes for credentialing, or integrations with EHRs — PHI could enter the system. A BAA in place from day one avoids scrambling.
2. **Physician trust.** Your customers are highly regulated professionals. A Supabase BAA (Supabase offers them at no charge on Pro and above plans) signals you understand their world.
3. **Audit surface.** If a physician complains to their state medical board or a hospital credentialing committee asks about your data practices, having a signed BAA demonstrates intentional compliance architecture.

**Verdict:** Not legally required; execute it anyway. Cost: $0. Benefit: substantial.

### 3. Does CredentialDOMD Need a BAA with Stripe?

**Determination: No — and Stripe won't execute a HIPAA BAA in any case.**

Stripe processes **payment card data**, not health information. CredentialDOMD's relationship with Stripe is a SaaS subscription transaction — not a HIPAA-covered electronic transaction (which are defined at **45 CFR § 162.1101–162.1801** and cover claims, eligibility, remittance, etc.).

Stripe's pricing and billing data is governed by **PCI-DSS**, not HIPAA. Stripe is PCI-DSS Level 1 certified, which is the appropriate compliance framework for this data. No BAA needed, none available, none relevant.

**One boundary to maintain:** Never pass patient identifiers or clinical context into Stripe metadata fields (e.g., don't tag a payment record with a patient name or diagnosis). Keep the integration strictly financial.

### 5. Minimum Compliance Posture for Launch with Founding Members

CredentialDOMD doesn't legally *require* HIPAA compliance. But your customers are physicians who live inside HIPAA and instinctively trust nothing that doesn't look familiar. Here's the minimum viable posture:

**Tier 1 — Required for professional credibility:**

| Item | Why | How |
|---|---|---|
| **Privacy Policy** (specific to credential data) | State law (CCPA/CPRA in CA, SHIELD in NY) and physician expectations | Explicitly state you don't handle PHI; explain what credential data you do collect, how long you retain it, and deletion rights |
| **Data Processing Agreement (DPA)** | GDPR if any EU physicians; general professional practice | Standard DPA template; offer on request |
| **Encryption in transit and at rest** | **45 CFR § 164.312(a)(2)(iv) and (e)(2)(ii)** (technical safeguards standard) — while not legally required, it's the floor | Supabase encrypts at rest (AES-256) by default; enforce TLS 1.2+ on all endpoints |
| **Access controls** | Standard SaaS security | MFA for physician accounts; row-level security in Supabase so physicians only access their own records |
| **Incident response plan** | State breach notification laws (**e.g., CA Civil Code § 1798.82**) apply even when HIPAA doesn't | Document your breach response; 72-hour notification standard |
| **Supabase BAA** | Positioning (see above) | Sign it on Supabase Pro |

**Tier 2 — Recommended before 50+ paying physicians:**

- **SOC 2 Type I audit** (or at minimum, a security questionnaire response document for hospital credentialing departments that ask about your vendors)
- **Penetration test** on the API layer
- **Vendor risk assessments** documented for Supabase, Stripe, Vercel
- **Retention and deletion policy** with a defined schedule (credential data has long shelf lives but physicians must be able to export and delete)

**What you don't need at launch:**
- A HIPAA Privacy Officer (you're not a CE)
- HIPAA training program
- Notice of Privacy Practices
- HIPAA Risk Assessment (though a general security risk assessment is good practice)
